Overview

In this post we explain how our imaging pipeline converts raw chest radiographs into model-ready tensors, how we perform inference efficiently, and how we produce saliency maps to help clinicians interpret model outputs. Our goal is to make each step auditable, reproducible, and robust to small changes in acquisition.

Preprocessing

We standardize input images to a fixed resolution (128√ó128) and apply normalization tailored to the model's training statistics. Steps include contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalization for improved local contrast, careful resizing that preserves aspect ratio with minimal cropping, and per-channel normalization using training-measured mean and standard deviation.

Inference & Efficiency

The inference stack is built on TensorFlow / Keras with an emphasis on FP16 performance for faster throughput and lower memory usage on supported accelerators. We batch small groups of images during offline evaluation and use single-image inference in the browser or on-device builds to preserve privacy.

Explanations

To help with interpretability we generate saliency maps (e.g., Grad-CAM) and overlay them on the original radiograph. These maps are a diagnostic aid and not a replacement for clinical judgement ‚Äî they help highlight regions that influenced the prediction, but they should be considered together with image quality and clinical context.

Safety & Evaluation

We calibrate probabilities and maintain fail-safe thresholds: low-confidence outputs prompt a 'refer to human expert' result. Our evaluation uses held-out clinical datasets, and we continuously monitor distribution drift to avoid silent performance degradation in deployed settings.

Closing thoughts

Our pipeline emphasizes transparency and reproducibility. Future work includes improved uncertainty estimation, better domain adaptation for different hospitals, and streamlined on-device explanations.
